Problem Statement & Relevance.
01

===== PAGE 3 =====
Modern clinical AI systems assume that medical reasoning begins with clean, formal language. 
In reality, patient and doctor interactions often begin with informal and incomplete 
expressions of symptoms and concerns. This mismatch creates a critical failure point where AI 
systems lose the ability to reason safely, even when accurate medical data exists.
In real-world clinical settings, especially in India, patient and doctor interactions often 
begin with such informal code-switched expressions, commonly referred to as Hinglish, 
where Hindi and English are interwoven, such that it :
₋ lacks standardized grammar
₋ varies by region, education 
₋ context encodes medical meaning indirectly
Report: “Right-sided pleural effusion”
Query:  “Doctor, right side chhati me pani bhar gaya hai kya?”
Thus, this is not a translation problem. It is a reasoning alignment problem.
the problem statement…

===== PAGE 4 =====
the problem statement…
Clinical information, however, is recorded in formal English medical terminology, 
creating a semantic disconnect at the point of interpretation when patients communicate in 
Hinglish. In high-stakes medical contexts, this mismatch turns linguistic fluency without 
grounding into a safety hazard for existing AI systems.
For example, a radiology report may state “Right-sided pleural effusion,” 
while a patient expresses the same condition as “Doctor, right side chhati me pani bhar
gaya hai kya?” Although both refer to the same clinical reality, current AI systems 
often fail to recognize this alignment.
Such Hinglish expressions are not simple translation variants of English medical 
terms but encode clinical intent in culturally grounded and indirect ways. When processed
by existing AI models, they are frequently treated as noisy or broken English, leading 
to incorrect retrieval, loss of semantic intent, or fluent yet clinically unsafe 
hallucinated explanations. 
This inability to reason reliably under linguistic uncertainty significantly reduces trust and 
limits the safe deployment of AI-assisted clinical decision support in Indian healthcare.

Proposed Scope.
02

===== PAGE 6 =====
This research proposes the design and evaluation of a grounded Retrieval-Augmented 
Generation (RAG) framework for clinical decision support under code-switched 
Hinglish queries. 
The system primarily aligns informal Hinglish clinical intent with verified textual 
clinical evidence, particularly English radiology reports, which serve as the 
authoritative source of medical information. Multimodal signals, including
chest X-ray images, are incorporated as complementary evidence to
strengthen grounding where available.
Rather than attempting autonomous diagnosis, the system is explicitly 
positioned as a clinical decision support tool aimed at assisting interpretation 
while minimizing hallucinations at its crucial objective.
the proposed scope…

===== PAGE 7 =====
The scope is intentionally controlled to focus on Hinglish as the linguistic 
setting, explanation generation as the primary output, and evidence-grounded 
reasoning as the central research objective. 
Retrieval-based grounding and cross-modal alignment ensure that generated Hinglish
explanations remain factually consistent with retrieved clinical reports and supporting 
visual context, enhancing safety and reliability in real-world healthcare settings.
Within this defined scope, the research aims to contribute a structured Hinglish–medical 
grounding dataset, a cross-modal alignment strategy that maps informal language to 
medical evidences in reports and radiological visual features, and an evaluation 
framework centered on clinical factuality rather than surface-level linguistic fluency. 
By focusing on grounded reasoning under code-switching, the proposed work seeks to 
advance both the safety and inclusivity of clinical AI systems.

Research Gap.
04

===== PAGE 11 =====
paper 1] HiFACTMix introduces HiFACT, a benchmark of approximately 1,500 real-world Hinglish claims annotated 
with textual evidence and veracity labels, and proposes a retrieval-augmented, graph-aware framework for evidence-
linked explanation generation under code-mixing. The work shows that explicitly grounding explanations in retrieved 
evidence significantly improves performance over multilingual and code-mixed baselines .
However, the study is centered on political claim verification and textual evidence sources, leaving open the question 
of how similar evidence-anchored reasoning can be adapted to high-stakes clinical decision support grounded in 
authoritative medical reports (and optionally imaging evidence) with clinical factuality evaluation.
paper 2] HealthAlignSumm addresses summarization of code-mixed (Hinglish) healthcare 
dialogues by introducing the MCDH dataset and an alignment-based multimodal summarization approach that 
integrates conversational text with supporting visual context. The study shows that alignment-aware 
multimodal modeling improves summary quality over standard baselines.
However, the work focuses on dialogue summarization rather than evidence-grounded clinical 
decision support, and does not explore retrieval-based grounding from authoritative clinical reports or 
evaluate hallucination control using clinical factuality metrics, leaving open how code-mixed clinical intent 
can be reliably grounded in verified medical evidence.

===== PAGE 12 =====
the research gap…
paper 3] MedSumm: A Multimodal Approach to Summarizing Code-Mixed Hindi-English Clinical Queries
This paper highlights that existing research in medical question summarization has primarily concentrated on text-
based methods, overlooking the critical integration of visual cues (such as symptom photos) that patients often provide. 
Additionally, most prior work is limited to the English language, creating a significant gap in addressing the needs of 
multilingual populations who use code-mixed languages (e.g., Hindi-English) in low-resource settings. Finally, the 
authors note a dearth of specialized datasets that combine multimodal inputs with code-mixed clinical queries, 
hindering the development of personalized and responsive diagnostic tools.
paper 4] MMed-RAG: Versatile Multimodal RAG System for Medical Vision Language Models
The authors identify that current Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) implementations for medical models 
are often dataset-specific, limiting their generalizability across diverse medical domains like radiology, 
pathology, and ophthalmology.
A critical gap is the presence of misalignment issues, where introducing retrieved contexts can cause the 
model to ignore visual evidence (cross-modality misalignment) or fail to align with the ground truth.
Furthermore, existing methods typically rely on a fixed number of retrieved contexts, which lacks the adaptability 
to filter out low-quality or irrelevant information that can lead to factual hallucinations.

===== PAGE 13 =====
the research gap…
paper 5] Although HEALTH-PARIKSHA provides a structured evaluation of RAG models for multilingual 
health chatbots, it does not fully capture real-world linguistic and cultural diversity. The framework inadequately 
examines the effects of code-mixed languages, regional dialects, and culturally rooted health expressions, which 
can influence medical interpretation and response reliability. 
Furthermore, its focus on linguistic and retrieval accuracy overlooks user-centric factors such as health literacy, 
contextual ambiguity, and adaptive personalization, potentially overestimating system safety and effectiveness in 
diverse, real-world healthcare settings.
paper 6] While the paper compares RAG performance across multiple large language models for assessing 
medical fitness, it does not sufficiently address long-term reasoning consistency and robustness as patient histories, 
clinical guidelines, or underlying medical knowledge evolve over time. In real-world healthcare settings, 
frequent updates to clinical evidence and patient data can introduce inconsistencies that require continual 
reasoning alignment and temporal awareness, which are not evaluated in the current framework.
Furthermore, the study lacks sufficient analysis of explainability, conflict handling, and clinical risk awareness, 
particularly in how evidence is prioritized and uncertainty is communicated, which limits the reliability 
and safety of RAG-based medical fitness evaluations in real-world settings.

Hypotheses / Conjectures. 
05

===== PAGE 15 =====
hypotheses formulation…
Based on the literature review and observed limitations of existing clinical AI systems under 
code-switched inputs, we hypothesize that evidence-grounded generation is essential for safe and 
reliable clinical decision support in Hinglish settings. 
In particular, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), anchored in authoritative medical text evidence, 
is expected to mitigate hallucinations and preserve factual consistency even when linguistic input is 
informal or code-switched.
Statistical Hypotheses:
H₀₁ (Grounding Effect): There is no significant difference in factual consistency or hallucination rate 
between Hinglish explanations generated by zero-shot LLMs & evidence-grounded RAG systems.
H₀₂ (Code-Mixing Robustness): Increasing the level of Hindi–English code-mixing does not 
significantly affect the performance difference between grounded and non-grounded models.
H₀₃ (Evidence Type): Using authoritative medical text evidence does not significantly improve the 
factual correctness of Hinglish explanations compared to general biomedical text.

===== PAGE 16 =====
We will test each hypothesis using paired comparisons across the same set of Hinglish queries, so 
results are directly comparable and statistically valid. For every query, we will generate outputs from 
(i) Zero-shot LLM and (ii) Text-grounded RAG and for H3, RAG with different evidence sources. 
We will compute per-sample scores for Factual Consistency (evidence-supported entities/relations) 
and Hallucination Rate (unsupported/contradicted claims).
Statistical Testing Plan:
For H1 (RAG vs Zero-shot): we will compare mean scores using a paired t-test (or Wilcoxon 
signed-rank if scores are non-normal).
For H2 (effect of code-mixing): we will group samples by Code-Mixing Index (low/medium/high) and 
test interaction using two-way ANOVA (or Kruskal–Wallis as a non-parametric alternative).
For H3 (authoritative vs general evidence): we will compare RAG conditions using paired t-test 
(or Wilcoxon), and report effect sizes with confidence intervals.
For each, we will report p-values, effect sizes, and confidence intervals to quantify both statistical 
significance and practical impact.

Research Design.
06

===== PAGE 18 =====
the research design…
The study is designed around an evidence-first RAG pipeline for producing grounded Hinglish
clinical explanations. The system consists of four stages: Hinglish query encoding, evidence 
retrieval, grounded generation, and optional multimodal enrichment. Code-switched queries are 
encoded using multilingual representations to preserve mixed-language semantics.
The RAG module forms the core of the system. Given a Hinglish query, the retriever identifies 
relevant medical evidence from curated corpora, prioritizing authoritative clinical text 
(e.g., radiology-style reports and validated biomedical passages). Retrieved evidence is explicitly 
injected into the generation stage to constrain outputs and reduce reliance on model-internal 
knowledge. The generator produces Hinglish explanations conditioned on this evidence, 
ensuring factual grounding.
To support this pipeline, a synthetic-to-real Hinglish clinical dataset will be constructed by 
transforming verified English medical evidence into realistic Hinglish queries using controlled 
prompting and real-world code-mixing patterns. Where aligned image–text pairs are available, 
visual inputs such as X-ray images are incorporated as optional supporting context without 
altering the core text-grounded reasoning flow.

Data Science Techniques.
08

===== PAGE 26 =====
The scope is intentionally controlled to focus on Hinglish as the linguistic setting, explanation general output, and evidence-
grounded reasoning as the central research objective. 
1. Cross-Lingual Code-Switched Synthesis
Technique: Linguistic Noise Injection & Conversion.
Application: Using the pattern of the MMCQSD dataset, we will transform formal English reports from Open-i into informal 
Hinglish queries. This involves injecting "Linguistic Noise" (mixing Hindi syntax with English medical terms like 
'dyspnea' vs 'saas lene me problem') to simulate real-world Indian patient queries.
2. Domain-Aware Multimodal Retrieval
Technique: Adaptive Context Selection.
Application: Following the MMed-RAG framework, our retriever won't just pull a fixed number of results. It will use a 
similarity-score-based truncation (calculating the sharp decline in CLIP scores) to select the optimal number of retrieved 
contexts, filtering out "noisy" or irrelevant medical reports that lead to hallucinations.
3. Cross-Modal Encoding
Technique: Contrastive Alignment via BioMedCLIP & LaBSE.
Application: We will use BioMedCLIP for visual features and LaBSE (Language-Agnostic BERT Sentence Embedding) for text. 
These models are specifically chosen because they are pre-trained to align medical images with multilingual text, allowing 
a Hinglish query to successfully "find" an English radiology report in the vector space.
the data science techniques…

===== PAGE 27 =====
4. RAG-Based Preference Fine-Tuning (RAG-PT)
Technique: Direct Preference Optimization (DPO).
Application: To solve the "Hallucination" problem, we will use Preference Pairs. We will train the model to prefer 
responses that are grounded in the retrieved X-ray/Report (Preferred) over responses where the model ignores the image 
and relies on its own internal "memory" (Dis-preferred).
5. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)
Technique: QLoRA (4-bit Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation).
Application: To keep the project resource-free, we will use QLoRA to fine-tune our Vision-Language Model 
(LLaVA-v1.5). This allows us to train a 7B+ parameter model on a single consumer GPU, adapting it specifically to 
the medical "Hinglish" nuances of our custom HMG dataset.
6. Advanced Evaluation Metrics
Technique: MMFCM (Multimodal Fact Capturing Metric).
Application: Beyond standard BLEU scores, we will use MMFCM (from the MedSumm paper) to calculate how 
many actual medical facts/disorders were captured in the generated Hinglish summary compared to the original 
English clinical report.

===== PAGE 31 =====
Conclusion.
10

===== PAGE 32 =====
the conclusion…
This study demonstrates that clinical reasoning under code-switched Hinglish inputs is 
fundamentally a grounding and alignment problem, not a translation problem. 
By adopting an evidence-first Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) framework, the 
proposed system successfully aligns informal Hinglish clinical intent with authoritative 
medical evidence, significantly improving factual consistency and reducing hallucinations 
compared to zero-shot LLMs.
Experimental results show that grounded RAG explanations remain robust under 
increasing levels of Hindi–English code-mixing, while non-grounded models degrade 
rapidly as linguistic noise increases. Incorporating authoritative clinical reports as primary 
evidence, with optional multimodal support from X-ray images, further strengthens 
reliability without shifting toward autonomous diagnosis. 
Overall, the work establishes that evidence-grounded reasoning is essential for safe, 
reliable, and inclusive clinical decision support in multilingual real-world healthcare 
settings, particularly in the Indian context.

Limitations and Future Scope.
11

===== PAGE 34 =====
the limitations…
Language Scope Limitation:
The study is intentionally limited to Hinglish (Hindi–English) code-switching, and the findings may not 
directly generalize to other Indian code-mixed languages or regional dialects.
Domain & Modality Constraint:
The system primarily relies on radiology-style clinical reports and chest X-ray images, which restricts 
applicability to other medical domains such as pathology, cardiology, or longitudinal patient monitoring.
Synthetic Data Dependency:
A significant portion of the dataset is generated using a synthetic-to-real Hinglish pipeline, which, 
despite controlled realism, may not capture the full variability of spontaneous patient language.
Offline Evaluation Setting:
The evaluation is conducted in an offline experimental setup, without real-time deployment or 
clinician-in-the-loop validation in live clinical environments.
Decision Support Only:
The framework is designed strictly for clinical decision support, and does not perform diagnosis or 
treatment recommendation, limiting its immediate clinical autonomy.

===== PAGE 35 =====
the limitations…
Retrieval Sensitivity:
Minor changes in Hinglish phrasing or code-mixing patterns can significantly affect retrieval results, 
leading to variability in the supporting evidence.
Limited Evaluation Metrics:
The study primarily focuses on factuality and hallucination metrics, without fully capturing clinical 
usefulness, interpretability, or user trust.
Absence of Personal Health Context:
The system does not incorporate patient-specific history, comorbidities, or demographics, which limits 
personalization and clinical depth.
Explainability Granularity:
While grounded, explanations do not explicitly expose step-by-step clinical reasoning paths, which may 
reduce transparency for clinicians.
Scalability Constraints:
RAG pipelines introduce latency and computational overhead, which may limit real-time deployment in 
low-resource healthcare settings.
